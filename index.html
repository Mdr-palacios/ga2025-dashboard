What changes in a midterm

Factors that reshape the 2025 → 2026 translation

Top of ticket

2026 ballot: Ossoff Senate re-elect, open Governor (Kemp term-limited), Lt. Gov, AG, SoS. Senate races in GA pull ~85% of presidential turnout vs ~22% for this PSC race — a fundamentally larger, less-partisan electorate.

Midterm penalty

The president's party historically loses ~2.4pp of vote share in midterms. With Trump in office, this tailwinds Democrats — but the magnitude varies enormously by suburban vs rural geography.

Drop-off bias

Young and Latino voters drop off most from presidential to midterm; Black voters and college-educated whites drop off least. The 2025 R-demobilization gap likely overstates the 2026 effect, since off-year specials over-weight the most habitual partisans.

Geographic shift

Exurban Atlanta growth — Forsyth, Cherokee, Hall, Henry, Paulding — is structurally reshaping the electorate independent of mobilization. Forsyth ranks #12 here on demographic underperformance alone.

Election rules

SB 202 effects, precinct consolidation, drop-box availability, and any pending voter-roll maintenance shape the 2026 baseline accessibility — particularly in rural majority-Black counties where infrastructure is thinnest.

Special-election caveat

Off-year electorates are not microcosms of midterm electorates. Use 2025 patterns to identify targets, but expect partisan retention gaps to compress as turnout scales 4× toward presidential levels.

How this projection works

Methodology & assumptions

Turnout target

57% of registered voters (~4.0M ballots), based on the 2022 (~57%) and 2018 (~55%) GA midterm baselines. Per-county turnout uses each county's own 2022/2024 history blended with the statewide target.

Partisan drop-off

Dem coalition drops ~22% from presidential to midterm (Black voters drop ~25%, Latino ~35%, young ~40%). GOP coalition drops ~18% (older, whiter coalition retains better).

National environment swing

Baseline applies a +5pp Dem swing on top of drop-off, matching the historical midterm penalty for the in-party (here, Trump). Stress scenarios add ±5pp on top.

County-level adjustment

Counties with high % Black or Latino population see slightly higher Dem drop-off; high % college-educated counties retain better. Heavily white non-college counties retain GOP votes better than urban counties.

Calibration check

The 2022 Senate (Warnock 50.4% vs Walker 49.5%) and 2024 Pres (Harris 48.9% vs Trump 51.0%) imply a ~3pp midterm penalty for the in-party. Our baseline reproduces an R+1 outcome — consistent with current polling.

Race-specific overlays

The Senate vs Governor split applies an additional +2pp Dem layer for Ossoff incumbency, with +1.5pp extra in suburban metro Atlanta (14 counties where Ossoff overperformed in 2020/2024). The Governor race carries no incumbency overlay and a wider ±3pp uncertainty band reflecting open-seat candidate effects.

Registration adjustment

Statewide registration grew +0.75% (+56K) from Oct 2024 to March 2026 — modest. The headline projection uses a 7.4M registered baseline; the live registration figure (7.54M) sits ~140K above that and would shift turnout-target ballots by ~80K, well within scenario noise. Per-county registration deltas are not used because only 5 counties have verified data.

Important caveats

This is a partisan baseline projection. It does NOT model late-campaign news, candidate scandals, or differential ad spending. Treat as a starting point for scenario thinking, not a forecast.
